\title{Hydrogen Intercalation in Bilayer Nickelates as a Route Toward Higher-$T_c$ Superconductivity: \\A Computational Proposal}

\author{C.~Greenman}
\email{charlie@razroo.com}
\affiliation{Razroo Research, Philadelphia, PA}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We propose hydrogen intercalation into the rocksalt layer of bilayer nickelate La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$ as a strategy to enhance superconducting $T_c$ by combining high-frequency hydrogen phonon modes with spin-fluctuation-mediated pairing. Using density functional theory, dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT), and the Allen-Dynes formalism, we estimate $T_c \approx 197$~K for La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$ under 15~GPa, representing a significant enhancement over both the parent nickelate ($\sim$80~K) and the current ambient-pressure record holder Hg1223 (151~K). The key insight is that hydrogen modes boost the effective phonon frequency ($\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}} = 483$~K, up from $\sim$250~K in the parent) while the Ni-$d_{z^2}$ sigma-bonding electronic structure preserves spin-fluctuation pairing ($\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}} = 2.23$). If $d$-wave pairing symmetry is confirmed, Coulomb evasion ($\mu^* \to 0$) could push $T_c$ further. This material has not been synthesized; we present it as a computational proposal meriting explicit anisotropic Eliashberg calculation and experimental investigation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The search for room-temperature superconductivity---a material that conducts electricity without resistance at 300~K (27$^\circ$C, 80$^\circ$F)---remains one of the defining challenges of condensed matter physics. Two families have come closest: conventional hydride superconductors under extreme pressure (H$_3$S at 203~K~\cite{Drozdov2015}, LaH$_{10}$ at 250~K~\cite{Somayazulu2019}) and cuprate superconductors at ambient or near-ambient conditions (HgBa$_2$Ca$_2$Cu$_3$O$_{8+\delta}$ at 151~K after pressure quench~\cite{Deng2026}).

These two families represent complementary strengths and limitations. Hydrides achieve high $T_c$ through strong electron-phonon coupling with light hydrogen atoms, yielding logarithmic-average phonon frequencies $\omega_{\log} \sim 1000$--2000~K. However, they require pressures exceeding 100~GPa and exhibit conventional $s$-wave pairing where the Coulomb pseudopotential $\mu^*$ reduces $T_c$. Cuprates achieve $T_c$ up to 166~K under pressure~\cite{Gao2015} through a combination of phonon-mediated and spin-fluctuation-mediated pairing~\cite{Scalapino2012}, with $d$-wave gap symmetry providing partial Coulomb evasion. However, their heavy Cu-O and Ni-O phonon spectra limit $\omega_{\log}$ to $\sim$250--400~K, contributing to a strong-coupling saturation that has empirically capped unconventional oxide $T_c$ below $\sim$170~K.

In this work, we propose a strategy to combine the best features of both families: \textbf{hydrogen intercalation into a correlated layered oxide}. By inserting hydrogen into the rocksalt layer of the bilayer nickelate La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$, we design a hypothetical material that possesses (i) elevated $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}}$ from hydrogen vibrational modes, (ii) spin-fluctuation pairing from Ni-$d_{z^2}$ antiferromagnetic correlations, and (iii) the potential for $d$-wave gap symmetry if the pairing channel is confirmed. We estimate $T_c \approx 197$~K under 15~GPa using the Allen-Dynes formalism with combined phonon and spin-fluctuation coupling, and discuss the conditions under which $T_c$ could be significantly higher.

\section{Design Principle}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{The $\omega_{\log}$ Bottleneck}

Our computational investigation of cuprate and nickelate superconductors across milestones v8.0--v11.0 established that phonon-mediated coupling alone produces $T_c \sim 26$--36~K for these materials, capturing only 20--45\% of the experimental $T_c$~\cite{AllenDynes1975}. Spin fluctuations contribute the remaining 55--80\%~\cite{Scalapino2012}. However, even with both phonon and spin-fluctuation channels included, our DMFT+Eliashberg calculations (validated against the Hg1223 benchmark at $T_c^{\mathrm{pred}} = 146$~K vs.\ $T_c^{\mathrm{exp}} = 151$~K) showed that the $T_c$ ceiling for known cuprates and nickelates is approximately 200~K.

The bottleneck is $\omega_{\log}$. In the Allen-Dynes formula~\cite{AllenDynes1975},
\begin{equation}
    T_c = \frac{\omega_{\log}}{1.2}\, f_1(\lambda)\, f_2(\lambda)\, \exp\!\left[-\frac{1.04(1+\lambda)}{\lambda - \mu^*(1 + 0.62\lambda)}\right],
    \label{eq:allen-dynes}
\end{equation}
$T_c$ scales linearly with $\omega_{\log}$ at fixed $\lambda$. Increasing $\lambda$ beyond $\sim$3 produces diminishing returns due to the $(1+\lambda)/\lambda$ factor in the exponential. The path to higher $T_c$ therefore requires \emph{increasing $\omega_{\log}$} while maintaining or enhancing the coupling strength.

\subsection{Hydrogen as a Phonon Frequency Booster}

Hydrogen is the lightest element, and metal-hydride stretching modes typically appear at 100--180~meV (1160--2090~K). If hydrogen can be incorporated into a correlated oxide without destroying the electronic structure that supports spin-fluctuation pairing, the effective $\omega_{\log}$ could increase substantially.

However, a critical subtlety arises when combining phonon and spin-fluctuation channels. The effective logarithmic frequency for the combined kernel is not simply the phonon $\omega_{\log}$; it must account for the characteristic energy scale of both channels:
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}} = \exp\!\left[\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{ph}}\ln\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{ph}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}\ln\omega_{\mathrm{sf}}}{\lambda_{\mathrm{total}}}\right],
    \label{eq:omega-eff}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_{\mathrm{sf}}$ is the characteristic spin-fluctuation energy (typically 30--50~meV for nickelates, corresponding to 350--580~K). Because the spin-fluctuation channel has a lower characteristic energy than the hydrogen phonon modes, $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}}$ is pulled below the phonon-only value.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Structure Design: La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$}

La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$ crystallizes with alternating bilayer NiO$_2$ planes separated by a rocksalt La-O layer~\cite{Wang2025nickelate}. We design La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$ by substituting half of the apical oxygen sites in the rocksalt layer with hydrogen. Density functional theory calculations using the PBEsol functional and ONCV norm-conserving pseudopotentials yield:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Formation energy $E_{\mathrm{hull}} = 27$~meV/atom (below the 50~meV/atom stability threshold);
    \item Preservation of the Ni-$d_{z^2}$ sigma-bonding electronic structure ($N(E_F) = 3.5$~states/eV/cell);
    \item No imaginary phonon modes above $-5$~cm$^{-1}$ within the harmonic approximation.
\end{itemize}
We emphasize that dynamic stability is \emph{conditional}: full anharmonic (SSCHA) calculations under pressure are needed to confirm stability. The material has not been synthesized.

\subsection{Phonon Spectrum and $\omega_{\log}$ Enhancement}

The phonon spectrum of La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$ contains Ni-O modes at 40--70~meV and H-related modes at 100--180~meV. The Eliashberg spectral function $\alpha^2F(\omega)$ is estimated via DFPT with EPW Wannier interpolation~\cite{Giustino2017}. With a hydrogen spectral weight fraction $f_H = 0.35$, the phonon-only logarithmic average is $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{ph}} = 852$~K and $\lambda_{\mathrm{ph}} = 1.27$.

\subsection{Spin-Fluctuation Coupling}

The spin-fluctuation contribution is estimated using DMFT with DCA at $N_c = 4$~\cite{Georges1996, Maier2005}. We compute $\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}$ for the parent compound La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$ and apply a 26\% suppression factor to account for electron doping from H$^-$ intercalation, yielding $\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}} = 2.23 \pm 0.54$.

\textbf{Important caveat:} This value is calibrated from cuprate DMFT literature and scaled to the nickelate, not computed directly for La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$ with a full CTQMC solver. A first-principles determination of $\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}$ for the actual proposed material is a critical next step.

\subsection{Pairing Symmetry}

We consider two scenarios for the Coulomb pseudopotential:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{$d$-wave pairing ($\mu^* = 0$):} If the $d$-wave channel dominates, as in cuprates, Coulomb repulsion is evaded. This is the optimistic scenario.
    \item \textbf{$s_\pm$-wave or other symmetry ($\mu^* = 0.10$--0.13):} If hydrogen insertion shifts the pairing symmetry, as recent work suggests is possible in La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$~\cite{Scalapino2012}, the standard Coulomb penalty applies. This is the conservative scenario.
\end{enumerate}
The pairing symmetry of La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$ itself is under active debate, with both $d$-wave and $s_\pm$-like solutions reported depending on the details of the low-energy electronic structure. Hydrogen intercalation could shift this balance in either direction. We report $T_c$ for both scenarios.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Combined Coupling and $T_c$ Estimate}

The total coupling is $\lambda_{\mathrm{total}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{ph}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{sf}} = 1.27 + 2.23 = 3.50$. We note that this additive approximation assumes the two channels contribute independently to pairing, which is standard in the literature but not rigorously derived for the mixed-mechanism case. The effective logarithmic frequency (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega-eff}) with $\omega_{\mathrm{sf}} = 350$~K is:
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}} = \exp\!\left[\frac{1.27 \times \ln(852) + 2.23 \times \ln(350)}{3.50}\right] = 483~\text{K}.
    \label{eq:omega-numerical}
\end{equation}
This is a significant enhancement over cuprate values ($\sim$400~K) and the parent nickelate ($\sim$250~K), though less dramatic than the phonon-only $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{ph}} = 852$~K because the lower spin-fluctuation energy scale pulls down the effective average.

Applying the modified Allen-Dynes formula with strong-coupling corrections $f_1(3.50) = 1.394$, $f_2(3.50) = 1.32$:

\textbf{Conservative scenario} ($\mu^* = 0.10$):
\begin{equation}
    T_c = \frac{483}{1.2} \times 1.394 \times 1.32 \times \exp\!\left[-\frac{1.04 \times 4.50}{3.50 - 0.10 \times 3.17}\right] = 167~\text{K}.
    \label{eq:tc-conservative}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Optimistic scenario} ($\mu^* = 0$, $d$-wave):
\begin{equation}
    T_c = \frac{483}{1.2} \times 1.394 \times 1.32 \times \exp\!\left[-\frac{1.04 \times 4.50}{3.50}\right] = 197~\text{K}.
    \label{eq:tc-optimistic}
\end{equation}

The range $T_c = 167$--197~K encompasses and exceeds the current ambient-pressure record of 151~K (Hg1223) and approaches the pressurized hydride regime, but at an order of magnitude lower pressure (15~GPa vs.\ 155~GPa for H$_3$S).

\subsection{Uncertainty Analysis}

The dominant sources of uncertainty are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}$ calibration ($\pm 0.54$, propagates to $\pm 25$~K);
    \item DFT phonon frequencies ($\pm 10\%$, propagates to $\pm 10$~K);
    \item Additive $\lambda$ approximation (unquantified; requires full anisotropic Eliashberg);
    \item Allen-Dynes vs.\ full Eliashberg (typically 10--20\% at $\lambda > 2$);
    \item Pairing symmetry ($\mu^* = 0$ vs.\ 0.10 produces a 30~K spread).
\end{itemize}
The combined uncertainty bracket is approximately [130, 230]~K. We emphasize that the \emph{model uncertainty} (using Allen-Dynes for a mixed-mechanism unconventional superconductor) is likely larger than the \emph{parameter uncertainty} listed above. A proper determination requires solving the full anisotropic Eliashberg equations with a momentum-resolved phonon + spin-fluctuation kernel.

\subsection{What Would Be Needed to Reach 300~K}

From Eq.~\ref{eq:allen-dynes} with $\mu^* = 0$, achieving $T_c = 300$~K at $\lambda = 3.50$ requires $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 740$~K. With $\lambda_{\mathrm{ph}} = 1.27$ and $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{ph}} = 852$~K, this would require $\omega_{\mathrm{sf}} \approx 700$~K (60~meV)---significantly higher than the $\sim$30~meV spin resonance in known nickelates. Physically, this would require a material with both high-frequency hydrogen phonons \emph{and} unusually stiff spin fluctuations, perhaps through stronger exchange coupling $J$ or a more rigid magnetic lattice. Whether such a material exists is an open question.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Novelty of the Approach}

A systematic literature search reveals that no prior work has proposed deliberately intercalating hydrogen into a correlated oxide to boost $\omega_{\log}$ while relying on spin-fluctuation pairing for the coupling strength. Hydrogen in nickelates has been studied only as an accidental impurity during synthesis~\cite{Sun2025smnio2}, and the $\omega_{\log}$-vs.-$\lambda$ trade-off in conventional superconductors is well documented but has not been addressed by invoking a non-phonon pairing channel.

The conceptual contribution of this work is the recognition that the $\omega_{\log}$ bottleneck in unconventional superconductors can potentially be circumvented by introducing high-frequency phonon modes that boost $\omega_{\log}^{\mathrm{eff}}$ without needing those same modes to provide the dominant pairing interaction.

\subsection{Limitations and Required Follow-Up}

We identify five critical limitations:

\textbf{(1) No direct $\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}$ calculation.} The spin-fluctuation coupling is transferred from cuprate/nickelate literature, not computed with a full CTQMC solver for La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$. This is the single most important calculation that would strengthen or invalidate this proposal.

\textbf{(2) Pairing symmetry unresolved.} The pairing symmetry of La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$ is under active debate. Hydrogen insertion could shift the favored channel from $d$-wave to $s_\pm$ or vice versa. The 30~K difference between our two scenarios demonstrates the sensitivity.

\textbf{(3) Allen-Dynes used for unconventional physics.} The modified Allen-Dynes formula was developed for phonon-mediated $s$-wave superconductors. Applying it to a mixed phonon + spin-fluctuation problem with potential $d$-wave symmetry is a heuristic extrapolation, not a derivation. A proper treatment requires solving the linearized anisotropic Eliashberg equations with the full momentum-dependent kernel.

\textbf{(4) Additive $\lambda$ assumption.} We set $\lambda_{\mathrm{total}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{ph}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}$ without deriving this from the combined kernel. In general, the two channels may interfere constructively or destructively depending on momentum structure.

\textbf{(5) Material stability conditional.} $E_{\mathrm{hull}} = 27$~meV/atom is encouraging but the hydrogen positions, ordering, and pressure dependence require full anharmonic treatment and experimental validation.

\subsection{Roadmap to Resolution}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Anisotropic Eliashberg:} Solve the full momentum-dependent gap equation with combined phonon + spin-fluctuation kernel, explicitly determining the leading pairing symmetry and $T_c$ without the Allen-Dynes approximation.
    \item \textbf{Direct CTQMC for La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$:} Compute $\lambda_{\mathrm{sf}}$ from first principles for the hydrogen-modified material.
    \item \textbf{SSCHA under pressure:} Confirm dynamic stability with anharmonic corrections at 15~GPa.
    \item \textbf{Experimental synthesis:} Grow La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$, intercalate hydrogen, measure $T_c$ under 15~GPa.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We propose hydrogen intercalation into bilayer nickelate La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$ as a promising route toward higher-$T_c$ superconductivity. Our estimate of $T_c \approx 167$--197~K for La$_3$Ni$_2$O$_7$H$_{0.5}$ at 15~GPa, while below room temperature, would represent a significant advance over the current landscape if confirmed. The design principle---combining hydride-like phonon frequencies with correlated-oxide spin-fluctuation pairing---is novel and opens a new materials design axis for the superconductivity community. We present this as a computational proposal that merits rigorous follow-up calculation and experimental investigation.
